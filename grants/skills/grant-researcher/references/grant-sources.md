# Where to Find Grants — the source ladder

Good grant research is systematic, not lucky. Funders cluster into a handful of types, and each type is reached a different way. Work the ladder below so you cover the field instead of returning whatever a single search happened to surface. Adapt everything to the org's **geography** — most funders only give within a defined service area, so a grant outside the org's region is usually a dead end no matter how well it aligns.

This list is deliberately geography-agnostic. The org's country/region comes from `ORGANIZATION.md`; the named portals below skew toward the US and international funders, so when the org is elsewhere, search for the local equivalents ("<country> government grants", "<region> community foundation", national lottery/arts/heritage funds, EU/regional programs, etc.).

## The five funder types (cover all that apply)

### 1. Government — federal / national
The largest pools, the most paperwork, the most competition.
- **US:** [grants.gov](https://www.grants.gov) (all federal opportunities; searchable by category/eligibility), agency sites (NEA, NEH, NSF, HRSA, DOE, USDA, etc.), [SAM.gov](https://sam.gov).
- **Elsewhere:** the national government grants portal and relevant ministry/department programs. Search `"<country> government grant" <field>`.
- Search pattern: `grants.gov <field keyword>`, `<agency> grant <program area> <year>`.

### 2. Government — state / provincial / regional / municipal
Smaller pools, narrower eligible field, **better odds** because the applicant pool is geographically capped. Often overlooked.
- State/provincial arts councils, humanities councils, health & human services departments, economic-development offices.
- City and county grant programs, ARPA/relief allocations, participatory-budgeting pots.
- Search: `<state/province> <field> grant`, `<city> community grant <year>`, `<county> arts/health/small-business grant`.

### 3. Private & family foundations
The core of most nonprofit funding. Reached by name, then verified on the funder's own site or their 990.
- **Lead databases (mostly paywalled — use for names, then verify on primary sources):**
  - [Candid / Foundation Directory](https://candid.org) — the definitive foundation database; some free tools (990 finder, nonprofit profiles).
  - [Instrumentl](https://www.instrumentl.com), [GrantWatch](https://www.grantwatch.com), [GrantStation](https://grantstation.com), [GrantForward](https://www.grantforward.com).
- **Free signals:** funders' own "Grants"/"Apply"/"What We Fund" pages; recent grantee lists (who they *actually* funded tells you fit); IRS 990-PF filings (a private foundation's 990 lists every grant it made).
- Search: `<field> foundation grant <region> <year>`, `<population> foundation funding`, `family foundation grants <cause> <state>`.

### 4. Community foundations
Place-based funders that pool local philanthropy — often the best fit-and-odds combination for a locally-rooted org.
- Nearly every region has one (e.g. "The [City/Region] Community Foundation").
- Search: `<city/region> community foundation grants`, `<region> foundation grant cycle <year>`.

### 5. Corporate giving & sponsorships
Corporate foundations and community-investment programs, plus local-business sponsorships.
- National programs from large companies in the org's sector or footprint; banks and utilities often have community-grant cycles.
- Search: `<company> foundation grant`, `<industry> corporate giving program`, `corporate community grants <region> <year>`.

## Turning the profile into queries

Combine dimensions from the search profile; single-word searches waste turns. Good query shapes:

- `<field> grants <region> <current year>`
- `<population served> grant funding <state/country>`
- `<project type, e.g. capital / equipment / operating> grant <region>`
- `<funder type> <cause> grant deadline <year>`
- `RFP <field> <region> <year>` and `"letter of inquiry" <field> <region>`

Run several angles. The best-fit local grants rarely rank on the first page of a generic search.

## From lead to verified candidate

Aggregators and search snippets are **starting points, not evidence**. For every promising lead:

1. **WebFetch the funder's or program's official page.** Confirm the opportunity is real, currently open (or upcoming), and read the actual eligibility, deadline, award range, and eligible-use language.
2. If the aggregator's deadline and the funder's page disagree, **trust the funder's page** — aggregator data goes stale fast.
3. If you can't reach a primary source that confirms the grant is open, treat it as an **unverified lead** — mention it if useful, but never score it as a candidate.

## Practical cautions

- **Geography kills more grants than anything else.** Confirm the org's location is inside the funder's service area before investing time.
- **Recent grantee lists are gold** — they reveal a funder's *revealed* preferences (org size, grant amounts, real focus), which often differ from the marketing copy.
- **Paywalls:** don't fabricate details from behind a paywall. Use the free abstract for the lead, then find the funder's own page.
- **Rolling deadlines** are worth flagging prominently — they let the org apply on its own schedule and score well on Readiness.
- **Watch for LOI-first funders:** many large foundations require a short letter of inquiry before a full proposal — note this, as it changes the Readiness lift.
