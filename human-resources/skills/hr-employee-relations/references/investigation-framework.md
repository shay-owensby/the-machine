# Workplace Investigation Framework

A workplace investigation exists to find out, fairly and defensibly, **what more likely than not happened** — so the client's HR and counsel can decide what to do. It is not a trial and its output is not a legal verdict. Done well it is **neutral, prompt, thorough, and legally-defensible**; done badly it creates more risk than the original complaint. Everything below is support material for the client's HR professional and/or employment counsel — never a legal determination.

> **Standing rules for the whole investigation**
> - **Not legal advice, not a verdict.** Findings are recommendations on a reasonable-basis standard for the client to decide on. Harassment, discrimination, retaliation, and protected-activity matters are jurisdiction-specific and legally loaded — scope to `legal.jurisdictions` and route to counsel. When `legal.counsel_review: true`, stamp the findings memo with the counsel-review banner.
> - **Confidential.** Keep everything in-project under `employee-relations/`, need-to-know only. Never invent people, allegations, or evidence — supplied/placeholder data only.
> - **Neutral.** No predetermined outcome. The investigator (or this support material) has no stake in who is right.
> - **Involve counsel early** for anything alleging illegal conduct, involving senior leaders, threatening safety, touching protected activity, or likely to end in termination or litigation. In a unionized workplace, honor CBA representation rights at investigatory interviews.

## 1. Intake — capture the complaint
- Record who is complaining, who is accused, exactly what is alleged (specific behaviors, dates, places, frequency), who else was present or knows, and what the complainant is seeking.
- Note how it arrived (direct, manager, hotline, third party) and whether the complainant wants confidentiality — explain you'll protect it as far as possible but can't promise absolute confidentiality when a duty to act exists.
- Assess **immediate risk**: threat of violence, safety, ongoing harm, or risk of retaliation. Take interim measures if needed (schedule/reporting separation, leave) — framed as neutral and non-punitive, not as pre-judging the accused. Flag safety/violence to the right internal parties and counsel at once.

## 2. Decide whether and what to investigate — and scope
- Not every concern needs a formal investigation; some are policy clarifications or conflict-resolution matters. But **any allegation of harassment, discrimination, retaliation, threats/violence, or other potential policy/legal violation should be investigated** — flag these for counsel first.
- Define scope: the specific allegations to be examined (and any that surface later), the policies potentially implicated, and the questions the investigation must answer. Keep it focused; note what is out of scope.
- Choose the investigator: neutral, trained, without a conflict of interest or reporting stake in the parties. Note when an external investigator or counsel-led investigation is warranted (senior-leader accused, high legal exposure, no unconflicted internal option).

## 3. Plan
- Identify who to interview and in what order — typically **complainant → witnesses → respondent → follow-up** (interview the respondent once you understand the allegations well enough to put them fairly).
- List documents/records/physical or digital evidence to gather and preserve *before* interviews may prompt deletion (messages, emails, access logs, schedules, prior complaints, relevant policies) — collect through proper channels only, never invent or illicitly access.
- Set a timeline — **prompt** matters legally and practically; delay looks like inaction and lets evidence and memory decay.
- Plan confidentiality and anti-retaliation reminders for every participant.

## 4. Interviews — neutral and non-leading
Interview each party separately and privately. Open by explaining the purpose, the expectation of honesty, the confidentiality limits, and the **no-retaliation** protection. Then:
- Ask **open, non-leading** questions that don't telegraph the answer or your view.
  - Neutral: *"Describe what happened on the afternoon of the 3rd."* · *"Who else was there?"* · *"What did you see or hear next?"* · *"How did you know that?"*
  - Avoid leading/loaded: *"He harassed you, didn't he?"* · *"You were fine with it, right?"* — these taint the account and the record.
- Go chronological, get specifics (what exactly was said/done, when, where, who witnessed it), and separate **firsthand observation from hearsay and from opinion**.
- **Complainant:** the full account and every witness/evidence they can point to.
- **Respondent:** put the specific allegations to them fairly and let them respond fully, give their account, name their witnesses and evidence — genuine chance to be heard (basic fairness and, where it applies, due process).
- **Witnesses:** only what they directly saw/heard; don't reveal more than necessary; note relationships/bias.
- Take careful, factual notes (or a recording only where lawful and disclosed — recording consent is jurisdiction-specific; verify). Read back / confirm key points. Note demeanor factually, not as a conclusion.

## 5. Evidence handling
- Preserve everything gathered securely in-project; maintain a simple chain (what, from whom, when, by whom).
- Distinguish direct evidence, corroboration, and contradiction. Look for what independently supports or undercuts each account.
- Don't over-collect irrelevant personal data; stay within scope.

## 6. Credibility & weight
When accounts conflict, weigh — don't guess — using recognized, defensible factors:
- **Plausibility** — is the account inherently believable?
- **Consistency** — internally and over retellings.
- **Corroboration** — do witnesses, documents, or records support it?
- **Motive to lie / bias** — does anyone have a reason to fabricate?
- **Demeanor** — used cautiously, never alone.
- **Past record** — relevant history, used carefully and fairly.
Assess each factor against the evidence; state the reasoning. Never resolve credibility by who is more senior or more liked.

## 7. Findings — reasonable-basis standard
- Reach findings on the **preponderance / more-likely-than-not** standard (the workplace standard) — *not* "beyond a reasonable doubt." For each allegation, state: **substantiated / not substantiated / inconclusive**, with the specific facts and reasoning that support it.
- Findings are a **recommendation for the client's HR/counsel to adopt or revise**, not a final legal ruling. Say so. Do not label anyone as having legally "committed harassment/discrimination" — that legal characterization is for counsel; state the facts and whether they violate **policy**, and flag the legal question.
- Tie findings to the specific policies at issue.

## 8. Disposition & recommendation
- Recommend next steps proportionate to the findings: no action, coaching, training, a policy fix, or discipline (hand to progressive discipline; termination hands off to `offboarding/`) — as options for the client to decide, with the trade-offs.
- Plan **closure communications**: tell the complainant and respondent that the process concluded and any outcome that appropriately can be shared, and **reinforce the no-retaliation protection and a channel to report any retaliation**.
- **Monitor for retaliation** after closure — retaliation is often a bigger legal exposure than the original complaint.
- Document the whole matter per `references/documentation-template.md` and retain it confidentially; note that retention periods are jurisdiction-specific — verify.

## Anti-retaliation (throughout, not just at the end)
Remind every participant that retaliation for raising or participating in a complaint is prohibited, watch for it, and act on it. Retaliation and protected-activity questions are jurisdiction-specific and legally loaded — flag for counsel.
