# Performance Review — Template

A reusable performance-review structure that works for **both** the employee **self-review** and the **manager review** — fill the same sections from each perspective, then the two are read side by side (and consolidated with any peer/360 input) at review time. Keep every rating tied to **specific behaviors and results**, not personality. Adapt depth to role and level. Store the completed review in the employee's confidential folder under `reports/employee-reports/performance-management/<employee-slug>/`.

> This is a **draft for manager and HR review**, not a final or published rating and not legal advice. Keep it factual, behavior-based, and consistent with how comparable employees are assessed — a performance record can become evidence in an employment dispute. Never invent results or ratings; use only supplied evidence.

## Header
- **Employee / role / level:**
- **Manager:** · **Review period:** _(e.g. FY2026 Q1–Q4 / 2026 annual)_
- **Review type:** self / manager / consolidated · **Date:**
- **Inputs used:** self-review · manager review · peer/360 (list contributors' roles, not gossip) · goal records · 1:1 notes

## 1. Goals vs. results
List each goal set for the period (from the goal/OKR framework) and assess actual results against it with evidence. Score silence as "no evidence", not as success or failure.

| Goal / OKR / KPI | Target | Actual result (evidence) | Status | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| _e.g. Ship X by Q3_ | _measurable target_ | _what happened, with evidence_ | achieved / partially / missed / dropped | _context: scope change, blockers, dependencies_ |

Assess **results and the how**: what was delivered, and whether it was done in line with the company's expected behaviors. Note goals that changed mid-period or were blocked by factors outside the employee's control — context matters for fairness.

## 2. Competency ratings (behavioral anchors)
Rate the role's core competencies on the scale below, and **anchor every rating to observed behavior** — cite a specific example, not an adjective. Choose 4–7 competencies appropriate to the role/level (an IC and a people-manager have different sets). Common frame:

| Competency | What "meets" looks like (behavioral anchor) | Rating | Evidence / example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Quality of work | Delivers accurate, complete work at the expected standard | | |
| Ownership & reliability | Follows through on commitments; owns outcomes | | |
| Collaboration & communication | Works effectively with others; communicates clearly | | |
| Problem-solving & judgment | Handles ambiguity; makes sound decisions | | |
| Role-specific / technical skill | Demonstrates the craft the role requires | | |
| _(managers)_ People leadership | Develops, supports, and gives feedback to the team | | |
| _(managers)_ Strategic contribution | Sets direction; aligns work to priorities | | |

**Behavioral anchoring rule:** for each competency, describe what the rating level looks like *in behavior* before scoring, then point to a real example from the period. "Strong communicator" is not evidence; "wrote the incident post-mortem that the whole team now uses as the template" is.

## 3. Strengths
2–4 genuine strengths demonstrated this period, each with a concrete example. This is not filler — it tells the employee what to keep doing and feeds recognition, development, and retention.

## 4. Growth areas
2–4 areas to develop, framed as **specific, actionable, behavior-based** development — not personality criticism. For each: what to improve, why it matters for the role, and a concrete next step or support. Keep it forward-looking and fair; avoid vague labels ("attitude", "not a culture fit") and anything touching a protected characteristic or its proxy.

## 5. Manager summary
The manager's overall narrative: how the period went on balance, the throughline across goals and competencies, and the focus for next period. Consistent in tone and standard with how peers are assessed. This is where goals for the **next** cycle get seeded (hand off to the goal/OKR framework).

## 6. Employee comments
Space for the employee's own response and perspective — their self-assessment highlights, where they see it differently, context the manager may lack, and their development wishes/career goals. The employee's voice is part of the record; leave room for it and don't overwrite it.

## Overall rating scale (define once, apply consistently)
Use one scale across the whole cycle and calibrate it across managers before finalizing. A common 5-level frame — adapt labels to the client, but keep the definitions stable:

| Level | Label | Definition |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Exceptional | Consistently far exceeded expectations and goals across the period; clear, evidenced impact beyond the role. |
| 4 | Exceeds | Regularly exceeded expectations on most goals and competencies, with evidence. |
| 3 | Meets | Solidly met expectations and goals — the healthy, expected outcome, not a disappointment. |
| 2 | Partially meets / developing | Met some expectations but has clear, specific gaps; needs focused development or support. |
| 1 | Below / not meeting | Consistently fell short of expectations against documented goals; triggers a support conversation and, where warranted, a PIP. |

**Rating is a draft until calibrated.** An overall rating is a judgment from the whole record, not an average of the boxes — but it must be justifiable from the evidence above and consistent with how comparable performance was rated elsewhere. Do not publish a rating from this template; it goes to calibration and HR/manager finalization first.
