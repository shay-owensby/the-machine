# Goal-Setting Framework — OKRs, KPIs & SMART Goals

Clear goals are the foundation of fair performance management: they turn "how did they do?" from an opinion into a measurable question. Set goals **at the start** of a cycle (not retrofitted at review), cascade them so everyone pulls the same direction, and track them throughout so the review summarizes a documented period. This reference covers the three common goal types, when to use each, how to write them, how to cascade company → team → individual, and how to track and score.

> Goals and their scores become part of a performance record — keep them factual and evidence-based. Store individual goal records in the employee's confidential folder under `reports/employee-reports/performance-management/`. Never invent results.

## Choosing the goal type

Use the type that fits the work — most teams mix them:

| Type | Best for | Character |
|---|---|---|
| **OKRs** (Objectives & Key Results) | Ambitious, directional progress; cross-functional alignment; stretch | Qualitative objective + 3–5 measurable key results; often aspirational (70% = success) |
| **KPIs** (Key Performance Indicators) | Ongoing operational health; steady-state roles | Continuously tracked metrics with a target/threshold (not a one-time achievement) |
| **SMART goals** | Concrete individual deliverables and development goals | Single specific objective with a clear done/not-done bar |

OKRs answer "where are we pushing?", KPIs answer "is the engine running well?", SMART goals answer "what will this person deliver by when?".

## Writing them well

### OKRs
- **Objective** — a short, qualitative, memorable statement of *what* you want to achieve and *why it matters*. Inspirational, not a metric. (e.g. "Make onboarding something new hires rave about.")
- **Key Results** — 3–5 measurable outcomes that prove the objective is met. Each is a number with a target, and measures an **outcome/result**, not an activity. ("New-hire 90-day retention up from 82% to 90%", not "run 4 onboarding sessions").
- Set the ambition level explicitly: **committed** KRs are expected to hit ~100%; **aspirational/stretch** KRs are healthy at ~70% — so a "miss" on a stretch OKR is not underperformance. Say which is which up front, so scoring is fair later.

### KPIs
- Pick a small number of metrics that genuinely reflect the role's health; more is not better.
- Each KPI needs a **definition** (how it's measured), a **target or threshold**, and a **cadence** (weekly/monthly/quarterly).
- Prefer outcome metrics; watch for gameable proxies and perverse incentives (a metric that improves while the real goal gets worse).

### SMART goals
Each goal is **S**pecific · **M**easurable · **A**chievable · **R**elevant · **T**ime-bound. A goal that can't be measured or dated isn't SMART — rewrite it until "did they achieve it?" has an evidence-based yes/no answer.

## Cascading: company → team → individual

Alignment is the point — everyone's goals should ladder up to the same priorities, and everyone should be able to see how their work connects.

1. **Company** sets the top-level objectives/priorities for the period.
2. **Team** goals derive from the company objectives — each team asks "what must we deliver to move the company objective?" (a company KR often becomes a team objective).
3. **Individual** goals derive from team goals — "what's my contribution to the team's objective?" — plus role-specific KPIs and personal **development** goals.
4. **Two-way, not just top-down:** individuals and teams should shape their own goals within the frame, not just receive them. Ownership drives commitment.
5. **Traceability:** every individual goal should be able to name the team/company goal it supports (and a few legitimately bottom-up or development goals that don't ladder up — that's fine, just be explicit).

Keep it lean: a few goals per level that people can actually remember and act on beats a long list nobody tracks.

## Tracking & scoring

- **Track continuously**, not at review time. Lightweight regular check-ins (in 1:1s) on progress and blockers keep goals alive and prevent recency bias in the eventual review.
- **Mid-cycle is allowed to change** — priorities shift. When a goal is re-scoped, dropped, or blocked by outside factors, **record why**; that context makes end-of-period scoring fair.
- **Scoring at period end:**
  - **OKR key results** — score each KR 0.0–1.0 (fraction of target achieved); the objective's score is a read across its KRs, not a mechanical average. For aspirational OKRs, ~0.7 is on-target success — don't treat it as failure.
  - **KPIs** — met / not met against the threshold, with the trend noted.
  - **SMART goals** — achieved / partially / missed, with evidence and any scope-change context.
- **Score the result, note the context, cite the evidence.** A goal score is an input to the performance review (see `review-template.md` §1), calibrated across managers before anything is finalized — not a standalone verdict. And a missed stretch goal or an outside-blocked goal is not the same as underperformance: judge fairly.
