# Performance Improvement Plan (PIP) — Template

A PIP is a structured, time-bound plan to help an employee close a genuine, documented performance gap — with clear expectations, real support, and honest stakes. Used well, it is a **support tool** that often succeeds. It is also one of the most legally sensitive documents HR produces.

> ⚠️ **Draft — requires review by qualified HR/legal counsel before use.**

## Read this before drafting — legal sensitivity

A PIP is frequently the paper trail behind a later termination, so it is scrutinized in **discrimination, retaliation, and wrongful-termination** claims. To keep it fair and defensible:

- **Factual and behavior-based only.** Every gap is stated as a specific, observable behavior or a missed measurable result with evidence and dates — never personality, attitude, traits, or anything touching a protected characteristic or its proxy.
- **Applied consistently.** The threshold that put this employee on a PIP must be the same one applied to comparable employees. Inconsistent application is the classic evidence of pretext. If you can't show consistency, stop and raise it with HR/counsel.
- **Not retaliatory.** A PIP that appears shortly after protected activity (a complaint, a leave request, whistleblowing) is high-risk — flag the timing to counsel.
- **Documented and dated** throughout, with the employee given the plan, a real chance to improve, and a copy of each check-in.
- **Counsel first.** Before this PIP is issued, it goes to the client's qualified HR professional and/or employment counsel. If it is on a **termination track**, that is not optional — flag it explicitly and coordinate with `employee-relations/` (for any related conduct/discipline record) and `offboarding/` (should separation follow). This template is a draft; it is not legal advice and does not itself decide any employment outcome.

Store the completed PIP in the employee's confidential folder under `reports/employee-reports/performance-management/<employee-slug>/`. Never invent the gaps, the evidence, or the outcome — use only supplied, documented facts.

## 1. Performance gap (factual)
State the specific gaps between expected and actual performance. For each: the **expectation/standard** (from the role, goals, or prior review), the **observed shortfall** with dates and evidence, and the **prior feedback** already given on it (a PIP should rarely be the first time the employee hears of the issue — no surprises).

| Area | Expectation / standard | What was observed (evidence, dates) | Prior feedback given |
|---|---|---|---|
| _e.g. Deliverable quality_ | _measurable standard_ | _specific factual examples_ | _when/how raised before_ |

Keep this behavior-and-results only. No adjectives about the person.

## 2. Expectations to meet (clear & measurable)
Define exactly what success looks like — the employee must be able to know whether they've met the bar. Each expectation is **specific, measurable, and tied to a date** within the PIP period. Ambiguous expectations ("improve communication") make a PIP unfair and indefensible — rewrite until each has an evidence-based done/not-done test.

| Expectation | Measure of success | By when |
|---|---|---|
| | | |

## 3. Support & resources provided
A PIP is a two-way commitment — the employer provides real support, not just demands. List what the company will do: manager coaching/1:1 cadence, training (via `training-development/`), tools/access, mentoring, clarified priorities, reduced conflicting load, accommodation review if relevant. If the underperformance may relate to a disability or a need for accommodation, flag it for HR/counsel before proceeding — that changes the legal picture.

## 4. Check-in cadence
Scheduled progress meetings across the PIP period (e.g. weekly or biweekly). At each: review progress against the expectations with evidence, document what was discussed, give the employee a copy, and record it. Adjust support where warranted; do **not** move the goalposts on the expectations themselves without documenting why.

Set the duration to give a genuine, realistic opportunity to improve for this role and gap — too short reads as pretextual.

## 6. Consequences (stated honestly)
State plainly what happens at the end of the period, for each outcome — the employee should not be surprised:
- **Expectations met** → PIP closes; return to the normal performance cycle (and monitor that gains hold).
- **Partially met / genuine progress** → whether an extension is available (decide consistently, document the basis).
- **Not met** → the next step, which **may include further disciplinary action up to and including termination**. State this factually and without threat. Any termination-track outcome must be reviewed by HR/counsel in advance and coordinated with `employee-relations/` and `offboarding/` — it is never executed from this document.
